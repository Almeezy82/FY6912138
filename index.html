Abstract

This paper will focus on the life of a star from birth to death, starting with a massive molecular cloud 
of dust and gas, followed by the protostar, a young star. Then going on to look at how different coloured 
stars differ, succeeded by the main sequence, the phase they spend most of their lifetimes in. Smaller 
stars would turn into red giants, while more massive stars evolve into red supergiants, and eventually 
go supernova, leaving behind either a black hole or a neutron star.

Introduction

13.8 billion years ago was the beginning of the universe, The Big Bang. All the mass in the universe was compressed into a single 
point, which it exploded violently, creating all the matter we have today including stars with star systems. 

The greater the mass of the star, the more extreme its conditions become, causing their evolutions to vary greatly. For context, our 
sun has a mass of 1 solar mass (Ms). This unit of measurement is used to better understand of the sizes of other stars, 
with the largest observed one, Stephenson 2-18, weighing over 2,000 Ms!

So, how did these stars form?

Analysis and Discussion

Birth


Molecular Clouds

We begin with the large molecular dust cloud. These are cold, massive clouds of dust that can span hundreds of light years and can 
weigh anywhere from 1000 to 10 million Ms (Nasa, 2025). 

Due to these large masses, the gravitational forces bring the particles closer together making the cloud denser. As particles collide, 
the friction between them causes temperatures to rise, resulting in the development of a protostar (Nasa, 2025).

Protostar

A protostar is a hot dense core that consists of a large portion of the dust compressed together, releasing energy as heat and light. 
It continues pulling in matter from the rest of the cloud around it, causing its size to increase as temperatures continue to rise 
(KiwiCo, 2025).

Main Sequence


It can take 10-100 million years (depending on amount of mass collected from molecular cloud) for a protostar to begin the main 
sequence, where stars spend the majority of their lives (LCO, 2013). Afterwards, they can either become a red dwarf, a low-mass star, 
or a massive star. The mass of a star determines its luminosity and therefore its lifespan, because if a star is more massive, the 
mass-energy conversion happens at a much faster rate, meaning smaller stars burn dimmer but have longer lifespans.

Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

The HR Diagram is a graph that shows a star’s relation to luminosity based on its temperature (Figure 2). It was found that cooler 
stars look redder and hotter ones appear more blue. 

Fun fact: Although Ejnar Hertzsprung in 1911 and Henry Norris Russell in 1913 both carried out the same experiment and obtained 
identical results, they had no knowledge of each other’s work. This level of reproducibility provided people with more credibility in 
this temperature-luminosity relationship.

Types of Stars


Red Dwarfs

Red dwarfs, the smallest main sequence stars, have a fraction of the Sun’s mass, with the biggest ones being ~0.6 Ms. They 
undergo fusion of hydrogen isotopes into helium, and will last for trillions of years because, due to convection, red dwarfs can burn 
out their entire supply of hydrogen. Although not yet observed, scientists believe they'll evolve into blue dwarfs, then white dwarfs 
and finally black dwarfs (Britannica, 2025).

Low-Mass Stars

Stars that weigh <8 Ms are considered "Low-Mass stars". They undergo hydrogen fusion at a much faster rate than red 
dwarfs, with lifespans ranging from millions to billions of years depending on its mass. When the star fuses all the hydrogen in its 
core into helium, the high pressures pushing outwards exceed the inward gravitational force, causing it to expand. Hydrogen fusion 
moves into the star’s outer layers as it evolves into a red giant (Nasa, 2024).

The red giant's core would undergo fusion of helium into carbon, and after ~1 billion years, the giant begins to pulsate and regularly 
ejects parts of its atmosphere. The dust emitted is called planetary nebula, which is dispersed into space, where it can form new 
stars or planets. Its remaining core would become a white dwarf (Nasa, 2024).

Supermassive Stars

Supermassive stars have a mass of >8 Ms. Stars of this size start to appear bluer because of how hot they get, as shown in 
the H-R diagram earlier. After its main sequence, hydrogen fusion is then carried out in the star’s outer layers, causing it to expand 
and evolve into a red supergiant. 

Red supergiants have enough mass to undergo fusion with elements heavier than helium, like carbon, oxygen, etc., with the heaviest 
being iron because fusing elements any heavier requires energy to be absorbed rather than released. This results in the core getting 
heavier, and once its mass reaches a certain threshold, it collapses in on itself due to its own gravity, causing the outer layers to 
violently explode out into the universe as a supernova (ESA Hubble, 2021).

Progenitors - What's Left?


Black Holes

When supermassive stars (>25 Ms) go supernova, black holes can form, posessing densities so high that once past the event 
horizon, nothing, not even light, can escape its gravity (Nasa, 2025). 

So, if no light is emitted, how can we see black holes?

They often have celestial bodies orbiting them because of their strong gravitational forces, allowing us to detect their influence. Also, 
they warp the space around them, giving them an outline of light from stars behind it.

Neutron Stars

Alternatively, supernovae can leave behind neutron stars. It gets this name from having gravity so strong that its protons and 
electrons merge to form neutrons. They're so dense that if you could scoop one teaspoon of a neutron star, it would weigh >10 
million tons! (DOE, 2022). 

Pulsars, another type of neutron star, rapidly spin and periodically emit beams of radio waves, as well as gamma and X-rays.

In Conclusion...

The mass of a star can cause its evolution to vary greatly, such as its lifespan, the colour of light it emits, and how intense its 
final stages are, which is why larger stars have shorter lifespans, but more dramatic endings as opposed to smaller stars.
